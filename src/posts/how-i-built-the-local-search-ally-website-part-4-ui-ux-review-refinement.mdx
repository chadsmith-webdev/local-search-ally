A website can look great and still fail the person using it. In Part 4 of this build series, I'm digging into the "invisible" work - the refinements that moved the site from "finished" to "professional."

When you're building for home service trades, "pretty" doesn't pay the bills. The site needs to load instantly on a job site, be readable in direct sunlight, and work for everyone, regardless of how they navigate the web.

> **Key Takeaways**
>
> - Moving H1 headings out of motion wrappers reduced LCP from 5.7s to under 2.5s ([Google Developers](https://developers.google.com/publisher-tag/guides/ad-speed-metrics), 2024).
> - Accessibility scores reached 100/100 by fixing heading hierarchy and color contrast.
> - Refined motion systems use `LazyMotion` to keep bundle sizes small for mobile users.

## Why was the page load "feeling" slow?

<Image
  src='/images/web-performance-audit.png'
  alt='LCP comparison showing 5.7s before and 2.5s after extracting the H1 from a Framer Motion wrapper'
  width={800}
  height={450}
/>

The service pages were beautiful, but the data said otherwise. Lighthouse was reporting a Largest Contentful Paint (LCP) of 5.7 seconds. In the world of search rankings, anything over 2.5 seconds is a problem.

I found the culprit in the animation system. I was wrapping every H1 heading in a Framer Motion `<m.div>` with `initial="hidden"` and `animate="visible"`. Even though the fade-in was quick, Google's bots timed the LCP from the exact moment the heading reached full visibility.

By removing the motion wrapper from the H1 while keeping it on the sub-copy, I dropped the LCP by over 3 seconds instantly. The heading now renders as static HTML, meaning it's visible the moment the browser paints the page.

Slow LCP scores often result from hiding primary headings during initial render. Research found that 53% of mobile users abandon sites that take longer than 3 seconds to load ([Search Engine Journal](https://www.searchenginejournal.com/website-speed-rankings/), 2023).

## How do you hit 100/100 for Accessibility?

<Image
  src='/images/accessibility-score-100.png'
  alt='Lighthouse accessibility audit showing a perfect 100/100 score after fixing heading hierarchy, color contrast, and ARIA labels'
  width={800}
  height={450}
/>

Accessibility (A11y) isn't just a "nice to have." It's about ensuring every NWA contractor, regardless of their device or ability, can read the site. I audited the initial build and found three specific failures that I had to fix.

### 1. The Color Contrast Gap

I was using a muted gray (`#6c757d`) for secondary text on our dark background. It looked "clean," but it failed WCAG AA contrast standards. I shifted the value to `#7a838d`, which provides enough contrast for readability without losing that secondary, subtle feel.

### 2. Broken Heading Hierarchy

A screen reader relies on headings to understand a page's structure. In the footer, I had jumped from H2 straight to H4. I corrected this to use H3, ensuring a logical, semantic flow from top to bottom.

### 3. Redundant Label Mismatches

I was over-engineering `aria-labels` on buttons where the visible text was already descriptive. This created "label-content-name-mismatch" errors. I removed the redundant attributes, letting the clean HTML do its job.

I've found that the more "complex" you try to make a UI, the easier it is to break the basics. Simple, semantic HTML is usually the most accessible solution.

## Re-thinking the Motion System

<Image
  src='/images/web-performance-audit.png'
  alt='Technical visualization of the motion system optimization using LazyMotion with the domAnimation bundle'
  width={800}
  height={450}
/>

Animations should feel like the site breathing, not performing a show. My initial setup used `whileInView` for everything. While it looked cool, it felt "finicky" as elements would re-trigger every time you scrolled up and down.

I transitioned the main page reveals to a single `animate="visible"` run. This makes the page feel stable once it's loaded. To keep the site fast for mobile users on job sites, I use `LazyMotion` with the `domAnimation` bundle. This ensures we only load the animation code we actually need, keeping the initial JavaScript payload as small as possible.

Many developers over-animate their sites, which can cause motion sickness or just plain annoyance. By favoring single-run reveals over repetitive scroll-triggers, the site feels more authoritative and less like a toy.

Every UI decision on this site is filtered through one question: "Does this make the contractor feel like the hero?"

Most marketing sites use "Sign Up" or "Start Free Trial" buttons. That's language for SaaS tools, not local services. I use low-pressure CTAs like "Let's Talk — It's Free" and "See Where You Stand." These are doors held open, not demands for a commitment.

## FAQ

**Why should I care about Lighthouse scores?**  
Google explicitly uses Core Web Vitals, like LCP, as a ranking factor. Research shows that 88% of consumers who search for a local service on mobile call or visit within 24 hours ([Think With Google](https://www.thinkwithgoogle.com/marketing-strategies/search/local-search-statistics/), 2023), so a fast site is directly linked to more calls.

**Is accessibility really that important for a contractor site?**  
Yes. Aside from being the right thing to do, accessible sites are easier for search engines to crawl. Almost 97% of home service consumers use Google to evaluate local businesses ([BrightLocal](https://www.brightlocal.com/research/local-consumer-review-survey/), 2024), and an accessible site ensures none of them are blocked from contacting you.

**Can animations slow down my SEO?**  
They can if they delay the rendering of critical content (LCP) or cause "layout shift." Using stable, single-run animations and keeping headings as static HTML prevents these issues while keeping the "premium" feel.
