## **Positioning**

**Positioning Statement:**

For NWA home service trade owners who know they need to show up online but don't have the time or know-how to make it happen, Local Search Ally is a local SEO and web development partner that handles their visibility so they can focus on the work they're actually good at. Unlike national marketing vendors that lock you in, go quiet, and move on to the next client — there are no contracts here. If it's not working, you walk.

**Key Differentiators (ranked):**

1. No contracts — zero risk to try  
2. Radical transparency — you always know what's happening and why  
3. NWA only — I don't take clients outside the region; this market is all I work on  
4. Full stack — SEO + web dev as one service, by one person who understands how they connect  
5. Accessible — reachable by text, not a ticket system

**Primary Competitor:** National SEO vendors and local marketing agencies (Hibu, Scorpion, Boostability, and similar) that sign contractors to long-term contracts, deliver little communication, and are unreachable when results don't show. Many NWA trade owners have already been burned by one of these.

**Secondary Competitor:** DIY / inaction — contractors who know they need to be online but keep putting it off, running on word of mouth until it slows down.

**5-Year Direction:** Services + tools. The brand is built as a *practice*, not a solo operator — so positioning holds as the business expands from done-for-you services into productized tools for contractors. The personal voice ("I") is intentional and a feature right now. As the tools layer builds, the brand will expand naturally without a rebuild. Think of it like a skilled tradesperson who opens a supply store: the craft credibility transfers.

## **Brand Script (StoryBrand SB7)**

### **1. Character (The Hero)**

NWA home service trade owner who wants more calls from people ready to hire — without chasing leads or depending on word of mouth alone.

### **2. Problem**

**Villain:** Invisibility. You do great work, but online you don't exist.

**External:** Competitors show up in Google's Map Pack. You don't. Customers searching for your exact service in your exact area never see your name. And when visibility is low, the default fix becomes paying Angi, Yelp, or HomeAdvisor for leads you should already be getting for free — what amounts to a recurring tax just to access your own neighbors.

**Internal:** It's frustrating — you know the work is good. You just can't figure out why the other guy gets the calls when you know you do better work.

**Philosophical:** The best contractor in town shouldn't be the hardest one to find.

**Compounding problem:** A lot of NWA trade owners have already paid someone to fix this — a national vendor or a local agency — and got locked in, kept in the dark, and never really saw results. So the invisibility problem didn't just stay unsolved. It came with a bill.

### **3. Guide (Local Search Ally)**

**Empathy:** "I came to this through real estate wholesaling — spent time in the contractor world and saw the same problem everywhere. Great work, completely invisible online. Then I'd watch the same owners pay a national marketing company to fix it, and nothing changed except they couldn't leave."

**Authority:** 97% of consumers use Google to find and evaluate local businesses (BrightLocal). The businesses in the Map Pack capture the overwhelming majority of clicks. Most NWA contractors aren't in the Map Pack — but the method to get there is well-documented and repeatable. Demo builds prove the technical approach. And unlike most people selling local SEO, I also build the site — because rankings without a site that converts are wasted.

### **4. Plan**

**Process Plan:**

1. **Audit** — Review local visibility, service pages, and competitor presence to find the highest-impact gaps.  
2. **Fix Priority Gaps** — Improve what matters first: local relevance, on-page clarity, and lead paths that support calls.  
3. **Grow and Track** — Monitor progress and adjust based on what improves visibility and call quality.

**Agreement Plan:**

* No contracts ever  
* Radical transparency — you always know what I'm doing and whether it's working  
* Monthly check-in, every month, no exceptions — not a report dump, an actual conversation  
* Reachable by text, not a helpdesk ticket  
* If I stop delivering, you walk

### **5. Calls to Action**

**Direct CTA:** "Let's Talk — It's Free"

**Transitional CTA:** Free SEO Audit Tool (built on the lsa-seo-audit skill) — earns trust, demonstrates expertise, and opens the door before asking for anything.

**CTA language (always low-pressure and specific):**

* "Let's Talk — It's Free"  
* "Start With a Free Conversation"

### **6. Failure (Stakes of Inaction)**

Every month you're invisible is a month your competitor gets those calls. Referrals don't scale. When word of mouth slows down, there's no system behind it. And if you've already paid a national vendor and gotten nothing — staying put isn't safe either. It's just familiar.

### **7. Success (The Transformation)**

Phone rings from people who searched for exactly what you do, in your area. Schedule fills from online searches, not just referrals. You can see which searches bring calls and which pages earn work. And you know what's happening every step of the way — because I tell you.

### **6 Structural Rules**

1. **Lead with the problem.** The contractor should see themselves in the first sentence — not your services.  
2. **Use "I" — always.** "We" signals an agency trying to sound bigger. That's not Chad.  
3. **If you can't name a specific number, city, or trade — the sentence is probably too vague to keep.**  
4. **Every CTA should describe the action, not the outcome.** "Let's Talk" beats "Grow Your Business."  
5. **Don't oversell size or tenure.** Honesty about being a startup is a feature, not a liability.  
6. **If a sentence sounds like it belongs on a stock photo, delete it.** ("Example: Empowering businesses to thrive.")

### **Drift Patterns (check every piece of copy)**

* **The rule of three** — "more calls, more jobs, more growth." One clear thing said plainly is stronger than three vague things listed together.  
* **Agency language sneaking in** — "powered by," "complete solution," "strategic," "digital presence." Name the actual thing.  
* **Vague specifics** — claims that gesture at proof without providing it. "Significantly improved visibility" — give a number or cut the claim.  
* **Repeated promises** — saying "no pitch / no pressure / honest conversation" three times on the same page dilutes all three. Say it once, well.  
* **Double adjective trap** — "good SEO deserves a good website." Reads like a slogan. Name what makes each one good, or cut it.  
* **Power word creep** — "dominate," "crush," "own the market." Sounds like a Fiverr gig. Use plain language: "show up first," "rank above," "get into the Map Pack."  
* **Hero language** — Positioning yourself as the hero instead of the guide. The contractor is the hero. You help them win.  
* **Vendor framing** — describing the relationship as a transaction ("my clients get…"). Reframe around partnership and accountability.

### **Examples — Do This / Not That**

**Write like this:**

"Most NWA contractors do great work — but they're invisible online. I've spent years figuring out how local search actually works. Now I use that to help contractors like you get found and get hired."

**Not like this:**

"We leverage cutting-edge local SEO strategies and digital marketing solutions to optimize your online presence and drive qualified lead generation."

**Honest confidence:**

"I'm not a big agency with decades under my belt. I'm a startup founder in Siloam Springs who's obsessed with local SEO, and I back everything I say with data — because right now, the data is my proof."

**Not hollow credibility:**

"As an industry-leading expert with extensive experience, we deliver best-in-class results for our valued clients."

**Naming the real competitor:**

"A lot of contractors I talk to have already been burned — signed a 12-month contract with a national company, paid every month, and never got a straight answer about what was actually happening. That's not how I work."

**Specific CTA:**

"Get an honest assessment — it's free."

**Not vague CTA:**

"Start your journey to online success today!"

## **Typography**

**Display / Headings:** Bricolage Grotesque (web) or Georgia (print/PDF fallback), weight 700

**Body / UI:** Space Grotesk (web) or Helvetica Neue / Arial (print/PDF fallback), weight 400

**Data / Metrics / Technical:** JetBrains Mono (monospace), weight 400–500

**Labels / Eyebrows:** Space Grotesk, weight 600, ALL CAPS, letter-spacing 0.15–0.2em

**When to use JetBrains Mono:**

* Scores and metrics in tools (e.g., "78/100", "STATUS: ACTIVE")  
* Audit data, percentages, and status indicators  
* Technical labels in dashboards and interactive tools  
* Code snippets or terminal-style output  
* NOT for marketing copy, proposals, blog posts, or social media

**Scale:**

* Hero/H1: clamp(2.5rem, 6vw, 4.5rem) — letter-spacing −0.02em  
* H2: clamp(1.75rem, 3vw, 2.5rem) — letter-spacing −0.02em  
* H3: 1.2–1.35rem, weight 500  
* Body: 1rem, line-height 1.7  
* Small/supporting: 0.875rem  
* Eyebrow/caption: 0.65–0.75rem, uppercase, tracking 0.15em  
* Data/metric: 0.85–1.1rem in JetBrains Mono, weight 500

**PDF font substitution:** Always use Georgia/Helvetica Neue in HTML that will be converted to PDF via wkhtmltopdf, since Google Fonts won't load in that context. JetBrains Mono can be used in PDF only if pre-installed or embedded as base64.
